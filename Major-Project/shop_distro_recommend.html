Recommended Distros for Beginners

Ok, but now you want us to recommend you some Linux distributions to use. Well, we got a couple for you.

All of these have the traits I believe are best for Linux on the page before and do have their own trade offs.

I am not being endorsed to support any of these distributions. I'm choosing these simply because I believe they're great and easy to use.

Linux Mint

Linux Mint is the ultimate starter distribution.

Designed specifically to help new users to the Linux world, Linux Mint is one of the best. It's a reliable operating system that's designed to be like the Toyota of the Linux world. It supports most types of packages, including deb, flatpaks, and appimage.

In addition to all these features, Mint is designed for beginners in mind. It's a good Toyota of the Linux world that's sure to keep going, even if you forget to take care of it. And when it does eventually have problems (as all computers do), the beginner nature means the community is going to be rather accepting to help you.

However, when it comes to desktop environments, Mint is really simplified on those options. They provide three out of the box options and nothing else. To be fair, none of them are a bad choice. But if you want to try something with a different visual look, the next option may be for you.

Check out Linux Mint

Ubuntu

You can think of Ubuntu as a more suped up version of Mint (although Mint is actually made from Ubuntu, it's a more stripped down version to help with simplicity). Made by Canonical, they really push their own package type called the Snap, which while technically universal, tends to be limited to Ubuntu machines.

Ubuntu is rather similar to Mint in most other respects. It comes with all your default apps, has a large userbase, and is rather reliable.

Unlike Mint however, Ubuntu is a far more open experience when it comes to how you install it.

Ubuntu allows you to choose from "flavors" as they put it, each providing a different desktop environment. For people that want a unique computer that feels like theirs, that's great. However, it is lower because of that.

The freedom of choice does come with it's problems. For example, I tried to use Unity Ubuntu briefly to get a screenshot showing how that looks. I've never used the environment before and came across a lot of issues: trouble finding pre-installed apps and trouble changing the theme to be something I barely liked when I tried messing with it on my own. While you can certainally get some good flavors like Kubuntu, my personal starter Linux OS, I would keep this in mind when looking through their options.

Check out the various flavors Ubuntu has, or check out Ubuntu's vanilla state.
