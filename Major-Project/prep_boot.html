Preparing your computer to boot

Depending on the age of your computer and what it likes, you may not be able to boot directly into Linux. This is because of a security setting on called Secure Boot that exists to prevent hackers from changing what your computer runs.

A little thing most people don't know is that your comptuer can't just directly go from starting itself to running an operating system. There's an inbetween step called a BIOS (or UEFI on newer computers) that sets up a good portion of your device. This is what also checks for secure boot to make sure your computer is running ok.

When it comes to entering the bios, you have just a few ways to do it, but today I will show you the most commonly used approach.

This is a good reminder that right now, you are going to be a bit of a hacker. While I wish there was a far less scary way to perform this step, there unfortunately isn't one that I am aware of. Thankfully, there are far harder steps in this tutorial and this one, besides the scary stuff, is rather easy and if you do it right, is rather safe to do.

Entering the BIOS

When you start up the computer, there's a few seconds as you start up the computer you can enter the BIOS menu. This button can vary depending on your computer in particular but it's typically an F2 or F3 key (sometimes you need to hold the fn key for the function key to work too) (if neither of these buttons work, look up the model of your computer and try that, or try the other method). This will open a menu that will vary greatly depending on the device.

When you're done, you should see a menu you probably haven't seen before and varies depending on the device. Often times, they look old and outdated interfaces, like something you would see on an MS-DOS computer. On some very modern devices, it may look like any other application you've used, but ever so slightly different. If you see this, congradulations and go down to removing secure boot!

I'm in the BIOS. Now what?

When it comes to navigating a BIOS, it can be rather scary. Nothing like looking at a very rudimentary display that looks extremely reminiscent of MS-DOS. This menu is designed for extreme tinkerers and isn't typically designed for the general public to mess with. Unfortunately for this process, you are going to need to be that extreme computer tinkerer. Scary.

The good news is that there are a few rules that can keep you safe. For one, if you ever believe you made any mistake in your settings, YOUR SETTINGS ARE NOT SAVED UNTIL YOU PRESS THE SAVE AND EXIT BUTTON!!! You can press either the esc key on your keyboard or your power button if you are ever afraid you messed something up you're not supposed to.

Speaking of messing anything up, your +, - and enter keys are your main keys for changing settings. I wouldn't touch them unless you're ready to change something.

I would also avoid changing any settings you don't recognize. While it can certainally be fun at times, they typically aren't meant to be accessed by most users so keep yourself safe.

With those disclaimers out of the way, your arrow keys are rather safe to press. Go and move over to the security tab (or if your BIOS is fancy, click it), and go to Secure Boot and press enter. You should either see another page or somewhere on screen you should see your Secure Boot set to disabled. If you're on another page, just hover on secure boot, press enter again and you should be all good.

Unfortunately, this is reasonably likely not going to be the last time you're going to see your computer's BIOS during this process, but this is certainally the end of you needing to mess with any of these settings. At this point, your computer is ready to boot a Linux distro.
